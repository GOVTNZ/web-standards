DSDS Purpose

To help government:

  • deliver services that are easily accessible, integrated, inclusive and trusted by all New Zealanders
  • become more responsive, open, citizen-centric and user-focused.

DSDS Principles

1. Identify your users and understand their ongoing needs

Who are the users? What are their needs? How will the service affect their lives?

In what contexts will people be using the service?

DSDS Principles

2. Be clear about what you are trying to change and why

What's the issue or problem being addressed? Focus on the benefits for people to help focus each stage of thinking and planning.

DSDS Principles

3. Integrate security and privacy proportionate to risk from the outset

(Explicit requirements)

Users expect to be able to interact safely with government and expect their information be protected appropriately.

DSDS Principles

4. Be inclusive, and provide ethical and equitable services

(Explicit requirements)

Make sure your digital service can be used by:

  • the widest possible range of people, including people with disabilities
  • people using diverse devices and technologies
  • people that can’t access it online for any reason.

DSDS Principles

5. Design and resource for the full lifetime of the service

Services need to be sustainable. Abandoned or unsupported services undermine user trust.

Old services are more open to security threats and may not be available in modern browsers.

DSDS Principles

6. Create and empower an interdisciplinary team

Delivering a quality service needs a diverse range of business and specialist skills.

Team members come and go, so plan around having the requisite skills in place at the right times.

DSDS Principles

7. Work in the open

(Explicit requirements)

Public services are built with public money. So work in the open and share code, etc. so people outside government can reuse and build on them.

DSDS Principles

8. Collaborate widely, reuse and enable reuse by others

(Explicit requirements)

The flip side of Principle 7. Work in the open.

DSDS Principles

9. Design for our unique constitutional and cultural environment

(Explicit requirements)

Respect the Treaty of Waitangi, and ensure what you build acknowledges te reo Māori, Tikanga, and Pacifika cultures. Also consider NZ's increasing cultural diversity generally.

DSDS Principles

10. Use digital technologies to enhance service delivery

(Explicit requirements)

Decide on and test technologies to be used so that they improve the service in a cost-effective way, and don't cause hindrances, e.g. incompatible with other technologies already in place.

DSDS Principles

11. Be a good data and information steward

(Explicit requirements)

Users need to trust that government will look after their data and information well. Lack of trust is a disincentive to citizens providing data and information willingly and openly.

Well structured and shared data also enable reuse and sharing.

DSDS Principles

12. Be transparent and accountable to the public

Taxpayer funded initiatives should openly demonstrate the value they deliver, and openly consider improvements, in order to justify the work and cost.

  • ‘umbrella’ approach that does not 're-invent the wheel'

DSDS Assessment Model?

4 possible options:

  1. Discretionary, informative resource
  2. Discretionary standard, self-assessment, self-reporting
  3. Discretionary standard, self-assessment, and centralised reporting
  4. Mandated standard with centralised governance model

We looked at what other jurisdictions already had or were developing, their approach for assessment, the differences, e.g. lack of central mandate.

Web accessibility

What is it?

Accessibility is typically understood to be about ensuring that people with disabilities and special devices or assistive technologies can access your establishment or website.

Percentage of people with disability, 2013

In 25 years, between 22–25% of New Zealanders will be 65+ years old or older.

National Population Projections: 2014(base)–2068
Statistics NZ

In New Zealand, based on the 2013 Disability Survey, 24% of New Zealanders have one or more disability. Note the high percentage of disability among those who are 65 years of age and older.

In 2014, 14% of New Zealanders were 65 or over. This number could almost double in the next 25 years.

Disability or some kind of impairment will be, for many or most of us, just a natural part of ageing, so while you or I might not see any personal connection to the need for accessibility at this point in our lives, do remember that more likely than not, we will as we get older. Be selfish about it!

Web Accessibility Standard

√ Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1

New Zealand Government websites are required to conform to WCAG 2.1 Level A and AA requirements. If you design/develop websites for NZ Government agencies, you should already be building to level of accessibility.

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1

  • Perceivable
  • Operable
  • Understandable
  • Robust

The Web Content Accessible Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1 from the W3C is a collection of testable requirements for building accessible websites.

How does WCAG translate to accessible content?

Answer #1: Well-written content

WCAG SC 1.1.1 Non-text content

All non-text content that is presented to the user has a text alternative that serves the equivalent purpose.

  • Images:
    • alt attribute for short description (~150 chars max)
    • long descriptions when alt won't cut it
    • see the HTML5 spec:
      Requirements for providing text to act as an alternative for images
  • Audio/video: captions and transcripts

WCAG SC 2.4.4 Link purpose (In context)

The purpose of each link can be determined from the link text alone or from the link text together with its programmatically determined link context.

Poor link text

  • Click here
  • Here
  • More
  • Read more...
  • Click here for more

Good link text

  • Find out about the Treaty of Waitangi
  • Visit Te Ara’s history pages
  • More about the Treaty settlement process
  • Buy tickets to Te Papa events
  • Search historic locations in New Zealand

Answer #2: Well-designed UI

WCAG SC 1.4.3 Contrast (minimum)

The visual presentation of text and images of text has a luminosity contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1.

Contrast ratio 11.6:1

  • Only 3:1 ratio for large text (~19px and bold, or 24px)
  • Text in logos or brand names are exempt

WCAG SC 2.4.7 Focus visible

Any keyboard operable user interface has a mode of operation where the keyboard focus indicator is visible.

WCAG SC 1.4.1 Use of color

Color is not used as the only visual means of conveying information, indicating an action, prompting a response, or distinguishing a visual element.

Especially links!

Here's some text with a link distinguished only by colour.

Visually indicate links in other ways. For example,

  • link with an underline
  • link with a highlight
  • link with contrast ratio of >=3:1 with non-link text

WCAG SC 1.3.1 Info and relationships

Information, structure, and relationships conveyed through presentation can be programmatically determined or are available in text.

WCAG SC 4.1.2 Name, role, value

For all user interface components:

  • name and role can be programmatically determined
  • states, properties, and values can be programmatically set
  • notification of changes to these items is available to user agents, including assistive technologies

Roles, states, and properties
