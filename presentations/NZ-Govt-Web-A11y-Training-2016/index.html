Web accessibility

What is it?

Accessibility is typically understood to be about ensuring that people with disabilities and special devices or assistive technologies can access your establishment or website.

Inclusive design

When I think about accessibility, I tend to consider it more as part of a broader social imperative to include all people, regardless of ability or technology, but with special attention being paid to the disabled, at which point we are really talking about universal or inclusive design, or inclusion in general.

A broad range of people use the web.

Accessibility / inclusive design benefit all of them
(and all of us)

When we think of it this way, as a matter of inclusion, we have an increase in scope, making it easier to consider the very wide range of scenarios and individuals, some disabled, some not, that benefit from accessibility. We are talking about all the people that our websites communicate with and serve, or should, and how accessible and inclusive design benefits them, but equally all of us and society in general.

Sensory

Vision Impairments

  • Blindness
  • Low Vision
  • Color Blindness

From experience, I'd say that while many more people than ever before have now heard of web accessibility, they tend mainly think of blind users, which is reasonable considering the highly visual and mouse-oriented nature of most web design and interaction.

But most people with visual impairments are not blind, but have varying degrees of low or impaired vision. Some individuals might have 20/20 vision, but lack the ability to clearly distinguish different colours.

Sensory

Hearing Impairments

  • Deafness
  • Hard of hearing
  • Tinnitus

Another form of sensory disability is hearing impairment, which ranges from those who are hard of hearing, to those who are profoundly deaf and who communicate primarily by means of sign language, which is a unique language quite distinct from the local primary written and spoken language. In terms of how closely connected language and culture are, many in the Deaf community consider themselves to constitute a unique culture or linguistic group.

Other people might suffer from tinnitus, or a ringing in the ears. This can be distracting, cause headaches, and make it difficult to concentrate, and listen.

Physical

Diminished motor control

  • Arthritis
  • Cerebral Palsy
  • Multiple Sclerosis (MS)
  • Parkinson’s Disease
  • Muscular Dystrophy (MD)
  • Paralysis

Then there's a whole range of conditions that involve motor control, which translates to an inability or difficulty in doing things like using a mouse or typing.

This might be caused by things like arthritis, or conditions of the central nervous system like Cerebral Palsy, Multiple Sclerosis, and Parkinson's. Or degeneration of muscle tissue as is the case with Muscular Dystrophy.

Others might suffer some degree of paralysis, partial or complete, that prevents them from using one or both arms. This can be caused by an accident, or a stroke, or as a result of disease. One can also be born with some degree of paralysis.

Cognitive / Neurological

  • ADD/ADHD
  • Autism
  • Down syndrome
  • Dyslexia
  • Dyscalculia
  • Learning Disabilities
  • Memory Loss
  • Photo-sensitive epilepsy
  • Traumatic Brain Injury

In terms of cognitive and neurological disabilities, again there is a whole range of conditions that can variously affect a person's ability to interact and communicate with the web.

Other

  • Repetitive Stress Injury
  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome
  • Fatigue, lack of sleep
  • Temporary injury

And there are a number of other conditions that we don't normally associate with or consider disabilities, but nonetheless can seriously impact one's ability to communicate, learn, and manipulate information and physical objects. These are conditions such as repetitive stress injuries, carpal tunnel syndrome, simple fatique or lack of sleep, and all kinds of temporary injuries such as breaking an arm.

In 25 years, between 22–25% of New Zealanders will be 65+ years old or older.

National Population Projections: 2014(base)–2068
Statistics NZ

In New Zealand, based on the 2013 Disability Survey, 24% of New Zealanders have one or more disability. Note the high percentage of disability among those who are 65 years of age and older.

In 2014, 14% of New Zealanders were 65 or over. This number could almost double in the next 25 years.

Disability or some kind of impairment will be, for many or most of us, just a natural part of ageing, so while you or I might not see any personal connection to the need for accessibility at this point in our lives, do remember that more likely than not, we will as we get older. Be selfish about it!

But, not just disabilities...

But accessibility and inclusive design are not concerned simply with improving things for people with disabilities. There are all sorts of other conditions and situations to which they are directly related and mean to address.

Cultural, linguistic, and generational differences...

Our communities are more and more multicultural.

Different cultures have different sensibilities to things like colours and certain phrases or uses of language.

Older generations can be less familiar with newer, everyday technology and how to use it.

Different devices...

There are too many devices to keep track of! Many people are still using older technologies that need to be considered. Some people can't afford or simply don't have access to high-speed internet access, and so they get online by using dial-up modems. Similarly, many people are unable to upgrade their computers, so are working with machines that may be slow or unable to provide a pleasant experience when working with some of the more current and resource intensive websites or software.

Different browsers...

Things are getting much better in the browser support space. Most current versions of popular browsers deliver a similar standards-compliant experience. It's not perfect--you still need to perform cross-browser and cross-device testing, but it's much better than it used to be.

Different environments...

Then there are all the different environments in which, largely thanks to mobile computing, people are able to get online and use the web:

  • in their car
  • on a crowded bus
  • in noisy environments, e.g., a concert
  • in environments requiring quiet, e.g., a lecture or theatre play

Why should we care?

But why should we care?

It's certainly easy enough, without even considering the question of disabled persons, to understand that it makes good sense to cater to the use of different devices in different scenarios: users are more and more visiting sites using different devices, and doing so in a variety of different environments as opposed to the traditional desktop home or office computer scenario.

But there are good reasons to be equally concerned with including or accommodating people with disabilities.

There is of course, the Stick.

NZ Human Rights Act 1993

Prohibits discrimination in the provision of goods and services on the basis of disability

The New Zealand Human Rights Act of 1993 prohibits discrimination in the provision of goods and services on the basis of disability.

And while this legislation doesn't specifically address online goods and services, it's not unreasonable, by way of what we call the “bricks and mortar” analogy, to consider your online presence as a simple extension of your physical one.

UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (Article 9)

2. States Parties shall also take appropriate measures:
a) To develop, promulgate and monitor...the accessibility of facilities and services open or provided to the public;
....
g) To promote access for persons with disabilities to new information and communications technologies and systems, including the Internet;

On an international scale, New Zealand was one of the first countries to fully ratify the UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (or UNCRPD), which explicitly addresses the provision of services on the Internet.

So the legislative stick, so to speak, is only getting bigger.

Litigation

Australia

SOCOG (2000)

Canada

Jodhan v.
Govt of Canada (2010)

United States

Under such legislation, there is the possibility that members of the public might take you to court if your website unduly discriminates against them.

This happened in Australia with the Sydney Organising Committee for the Olympic Games website, which was inaccessible to the blind. SOCOG lost and was forced to fix their site and pay money.

In 2010, the Canadian Government was found guilty of not providing “equitable access to all content on Government of Canada websites.”

In the US, all sorts of businesses and organisations are being sued because their websites are inaccessible.

While this hasn't happened everywhere, it's very possibly only a matter of time.

But there are also a number of more positive reasons to make your sites and services accessible. These we can call the “carrots”.

The one that most often gets pitched to commercial clients is the better business case, but this applies equally to government organisations.

We saw the numbers: an organisation can reach a significantly larger market if it considers the disabled.

Accessible sites are easier for search engines to harvest.

The cost of fixing bugs after a product has shipped is orders of magnitude greater than fixing it up front during development. Similarly, building an accessible site from the beginning drastically reduces the costs of adding accessibility after the fact.

Accessible sites tend to be easier for everyone to use. Studies have shown significant increases in conversion rates with all users when sites are accessible.

And even if what your website provides targets people that don't have disabilities, don't forget that their family and friends can be blind, have low vision, a motor impairment, reading/learning difficulties, use English as a second language, come from a different culture. If they can't access your site, they can't buy or use what you are offering.

So it makes good business sense.

Empower and Enable Individuals

  • reading and learning
  • playing
  • working
  • shopping
  • communicating with friends and family
  • participating in community

A more socially conscious reason for building inclusive websites is that they offer individuals, especially those with disabilities, the opportunity to be independent, productive human beings, to participate in the world, to do all those everyday things that most of us take for granted.

Empower and Enable
Community and Society

Inclusion:

  • helps society increase its potential
  • increases cooperation, creativity, productivity
  • enables greater civic and political participation
  • spurs innovation

Inclusive design allows more people to participate, which serves to provide all our social, civic, and technological discussions with a greater number of perspectives we wouldn't otherwise have.

Inclusion also, and importantly, serves to spur innovation.

Empower and enable
design

Constraints are fundamental to
design and all creative work.

In the early 19th century, Pellegrino Turri invented the typewriter for a blind friend and lover so that she could write love letters that were legible.

In the late 19th century, Alexander Graham Bell, through working to help members of the deaf community hear better, ended up inventing the telephone. This led in turn to the invention of things like speech recognition, speech synthesis, stereophonic recording and the transistor.

A more recent and everyday example of this effect is the OXO Good Grips line of kitchen utensils. Originally designed to help arthritis patients, it was found that everyone appreciates tools that are easier to grasp and handle. They were a huge success and are now one of the more popular brands of kitchen utensils.

Vint Cerf, inventor of the internet, has been hard-of-hearing since birth, and married a Deaf woman. Even in the early 70s, they were communicating by text message. This led to the development of email, which was integrated into ARPANET, what eventually became the internet we have now.

In all these cases we have a scenario where, in an attempt to overcome the barriers associated with a certain disability, a truly innovative technological advance occurred that changed things for all of us and enabled significant outcomes for society as a whole. This “side-effect” of accessible design, is something we call the “curb-cut effect”, and is a great example of how accessibility contributes to inclusion.

If you do one thing...

Make sure it works with keyboard only!

So many assistive technologies like screen readers and switches and voice recognition translate their commands into keyboard equivalent commands that if you do this one thing, make sure everything on your site that works with a mouse also works with a keyboard, you'll catch a whole number of simple access issues. It's not guaranteed, but your site will also me more likely to work in touch interfaces.

How is web content made accessible?

Answer #1: Well-written content

WCAG SC 1.1.1 Non-text content

All non-text content that is presented to the user has a text alternative that serves the equivalent purpose.

  • Images:
    • alt attribute for short description (~150 chars max)
    • long descriptions when alt won't cut it
    • see the HTML5 spec:
      Requirements for providing text to act as an alternative for images
  • Audio/video: captions and transcripts

WCAG SC 2.4.4 Link purpose (In context)

The purpose of each link can be determined from the link text alone or from the link text together with its programmatically determined link context.

Poor link text

  • Click here
  • Here
  • More
  • Read more...
  • Click here for more

Good link text

  • Find out about the Treaty of Waitangi
  • Visit Te Ara’s history pages
  • More about the Treaty settlement process
  • Buy tickets to Te Papa events
  • Search historic locations in New Zealand

Add keyboard support

  1. add handler for Space key

      $(this).click();

Add role and state

  1. add role="checkbox" and @aria-checked
 
<div class="checkbox" tabindex="0" 
   role="checkbox" aria-checked="false">Dogs</div>
  1. add toggle for @aria-checked

$('.checkbox').on('click', function() {
   if ($(this).attr('aria-checked') == 'false') {
      $(this).attr('aria-checked', 'true');
   } else {
      $(this).attr('aria-checked', 'false');
   }
});

Don't override native HTML semantics, unless absolutely necessary.
